Research

The redshift evolution of stars in a simulated galaxy from FIRE (face-on view).

I work with Prof. Andrew Wetzel as part of the FIRE collaboration, using cosmological zoom-in simulations to study how Milky Way-mass galaxies form and evolve. I'm interested in the dynamical evolution of stars in galactic disks, particularly how their orbits evolve over time, what drives those changes, and how they shape the structure we see in the Milky Way today. I'm also interested in understanding what drives the formation and settling of galactic disks.

My work is motivated by the goals of galactic archaeology, which seeks to reconstruct the Milky Way's formation history using the stars we observe today. Galaxies were much messier, thicker, and more turbulent in the past than they are now, and as they evolve, they gradually build thinner, more rotationally supported disks, while a variety of dynamical processes continually perturb stellar orbits. As a result, it is difficult to disentangle the conditions under which a star formed from all of the dynamical evolution that shaped its present-day orbit. My research uses cosmological simulations to better understand these processes, helping us connect the present-day Milky Way to its formation history.
